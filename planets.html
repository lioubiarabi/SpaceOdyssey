Planets

Worlds on our roadmap:
past, present, and future missions

Earth

The largest and densest terrestrial planet, balanced by vast oceans covering 71% of its surface, a dynamic atmosphere of 78% Nitrogen and 21% Oxygen, and a complex weather system.

Mercury

It experiences the most extreme temperature swings in the Solar System, ranging from 430°C (800°F) on the day side to −180°C (−290°F) on the night side.

Venus

CO2 traps heat, resulting in a constant surface temperature of about 475°C (900°F). Venus spins incredibly slowly and retrograde, making its day longer than its year.

Mars

A reddish planet with a thin CO2 atmosphere, vast canyons, towering volcanoes, and polar caps made of water ice and frozen CO2.

Jupiter

A gas giant with storms and multicolored bands, likely with a core surrounded by liquid metallic hydrogen, and the iconic Great Red Spot.

Saturn

The second‑largest planet and the least dense; it is a gas giant composed mostly of hydrogen and helium with a very dynamic windy atmosphere.

Uranus

A pale blue‑green giant with a unique axial tilt that drives extreme seasons; its color comes from methane absorbing red light.

Neptune

A stormy world with winds up to 2100 km/h and a faint ring system; its major moon Triton orbits retrograde.

Moon

Earth’s only permanent natural satellite and the fifth largest in the Solar System, crucial for stabilizing climate and creating ocean tides.
